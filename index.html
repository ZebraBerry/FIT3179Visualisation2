Each year, there are around 1,100 to 1,300 fatalities each year on Australian roads.

States with larger populations saw a higher number of fatalities, while less populated states have few fatalities. Notably, New South Wales (NSW), the Australian Capital Territory (ACT) and Victoria (Vic) saw a decrease in fatalities in 2020 and 2021, likely due to COVID-19 lockdowns.

The changes in fatalities each year was matched by the change in fatality rate which is normalised by 100,000 people. The states larger populations had the highest fatalities but also had the lowest fatality rates. Interestingly, the Northern Territory (NT) consistently had the highest fatality rate despite its relatively small population of 250,000.

Fatalities Affected By The Time Of Week

It is important to have an increased awareness during your weekly commute and the weekend travel which are times with higher fatalities.

The highest frequency of fatalities at 113 occur at 3pm on Friday afternoons, whilst the fewest fatalities of only 12 are recorded at 2am on Wednesday morning.

During weekdays, there are fewer fatalities in the early morning. As people begin to wake and start their the commute to work and school, the number of fatalities begins to increase. The increase in fatalities can be attributed to the increase in traffic on the roads. The number of fatalities begin to decrease again in the evening.

At the end of the week, Friday evenings have higher fatalities compared to earlier in the week since people are tend to stay out later, socialising and enjoying the end of the week.

On the weekends, the higher fatalities occur over a longer time period since people are embarking on road trips, visiting friends and family or participating in recreational activities
